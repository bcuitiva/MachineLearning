Module 02 · Linear Regression

Weight Prediction

Predicting body weight from personal, dietary and lifestyle variables using Ordinary Least Squares regression

01 — Problem Description

What problem does this model solve?

Obesity and excess body weight are among the most significant public health challenges worldwide, contributing to cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and reduced life expectancy. Monitoring and predicting body weight from lifestyle and dietary habits enables early identification of risk factors.

This model applies Linear Regression to predict a person's body weight (in kg) from a set of 15 input variables covering personal characteristics, eating habits, and lifestyle. The goal is to estimate a continuous numerical value — weight — based on patterns learned from real survey data collected from individuals in Mexico, Peru, and Colombia.

Why Linear Regression?

Linear Regression models the relationship between input variables and a continuous output by fitting a linear equation to the observed data. It is interpretable, computationally efficient, and well-suited for establishing a baseline prediction when the target variable is numerical — as is the case with body weight in kilograms.

02 — Dataset

Obesity Dataset — Raw & Synthetic

The dataset was created from survey data collected in Mexico, Peru, and Colombia, supplemented with synthetically generated records using SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) to balance class distribution. It covers individuals between the ages of 14 and 61.

04 — Data Preparation

01
Target separation The Weight column was isolated as the target variable (y). The NObeyesdad column (obesity category) was also removed from inputs to avoid data leakage, since it is derived from weight.
02
Categorical encoding — OneHotEncoder All categorical columns (Gender, FAVC, CAEC, SMOKE, SCC, CALC, MTRANS, family_history) were transformed using OneHotEncoding with drop='first' to avoid multicollinearity. This converts each category into binary columns.
03
ColumnTransformer pipeline A ColumnTransformer was used to apply OneHotEncoding only to categorical columns while passing numeric columns through unchanged (remainder='passthrough').
04
Train / Test split The data was split into 80% training (1,688 records) and 20% test (423 records) using train_test_split with random_state=42 for reproducibility.
05
Model training Scikit-learn's LinearRegression (Ordinary Least Squares) was fitted on the training set. The model learns coefficients for each encoded feature that minimize the sum of squared residuals.

07 — Interpretation of Results

What do the metrics mean?

The R² score of 0.579 indicates that the model captures a moderate portion of the variability in body weight. While the fit is not perfect, this is expected given that weight is influenced by genetic, hormonal, and metabolic factors not captured in the survey variables.

The MAE of 13.82 kg means that on average, the model's estimate is within about 14 kg of the actual weight — acceptable for a first-order predictive baseline, but insufficient for clinical-grade precision.

Key observations from the scatter plot

  • Points close to the diagonal line represent accurate predictions.
  • The model tends to overestimate low weights and underestimate very high weights, a common pattern in linear regression when the relationship is not perfectly linear.
  • Height and family history with overweight are the strongest predictors in this dataset.
  • A non-linear model (e.g. Random Forest, Gradient Boosting) would likely improve performance significantly.

Limitations

  • Linear Regression assumes a linear relationship between inputs and weight, which may not hold across all weight ranges.
  • The model does not account for muscle mass, body composition, or genetic factors.
  • A large portion of the dataset was synthetically generated, which may introduce bias in the learned patterns.
