Machine Learning in Healthcare

Heart Disease Risk Prediction

Heart diseases are one of the leading causes of death worldwide. Early detection of cardiovascular risk helps prevent serious complications such as heart attacks, heart failure, or strokes.

What is Machine Learning in Medicine?

Machine Learning is a branch of artificial intelligence that allows systems to automatically learn from historical data without being explicitly programmed with rules.

In the medical field, these models analyze large amounts of clinical information to identify patterns that may indicate the presence or risk of a disease.

How Does the Prediction Model Work?

The model receives different medical variables from the patient as input data. Based on thousands of previous cases, the algorithm learns which combinations of factors increase the probability of heart disease.

Once trained, the system can analyze new data and predict whether the patient has a High Risk or Low Risk.

Variables Analyzed by the System:

  • Patient age
  • Systolic blood pressure
  • Cholesterol level
  • Maximum heart rate achieved
  • Blood glucose level
  • Body Mass Index (BMI)

Type of Model Used

This project uses a supervised classification model. The algorithm learns from labeled data where it is known whether the patient had heart disease or not.

For implementation, Logistic Regression can be used, a mathematical model that calculates the probability of a specific event occurring — in this case, the development of heart disease.

Importance of Early Prediction

Early identification of risk allows the application of preventive treatments, lifestyle changes, and timely medical follow-up.

Additionally, the use of Machine Learning in medicine contributes to personalized healthcare, where each patient receives recommendations based on their own clinical data.

Real-World Applications

Currently, hospitals and research centers use artificial intelligence models to support diagnoses, analyze medical images, and predict risks of chronic diseases.

These systems do not replace doctors, but they serve as decision-support tools to improve clinical decision-making.
