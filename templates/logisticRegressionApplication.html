Module 03 · Logistic Regression

Diabetes Prediction

Classifying diabetes risk from clinical measurements using Logistic Regression

01 — Problem Description

What problem does this model solve?

Diabetes is one of the most prevalent chronic diseases worldwide, affecting hundreds of millions of people. Early detection is critical — it enables timely intervention, lifestyle changes, and medical treatment that can prevent or significantly delay serious complications such as kidney failure, blindness, and cardiovascular disease.

This model applies Logistic Regression to predict whether a patient is likely to have diabetes, based on 8 clinical measurements. The output is a binary classification: 1 (diabetic) or 0 (not diabetic), derived from the estimated probability returned by the sigmoid function.

Why Logistic Regression?

Logistic Regression is well-suited for binary medical classification tasks. It is interpretable — clinicians can understand how each variable contributes to the risk score. It outputs calibrated probabilities, which are more informative than a simple yes/no label, and it performs robustly even on moderately sized datasets like this one.

02 — Dataset

Pima Indians Diabetes Dataset

This dataset originates from the National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases. It contains diagnostic measurements from female patients of Pima Indian heritage, aged 21 and older. All patients were tested for diabetes according to WHO criteria.

04 — Data Preparation

01
Target separation The Outcome column was isolated as the target variable (y). The remaining 8 clinical columns formed the feature matrix (X).
02
Train / Test split The dataset was split into 80% training (614 records) and 20% test (154 records) using train_test_split with random_state=42 for reproducibility.
03
Feature scaling — StandardScaler All features were standardized using StandardScaler (zero mean, unit variance). The scaler was fitted exclusively on the training set and then applied to both train and test sets to prevent data leakage. Scaling is essential for Logistic Regression because it uses gradient-based optimization that is sensitive to feature magnitude.
04
Model training Scikit-learn's LogisticRegression was fitted on the scaled training set. The model learns one coefficient per feature, estimating the log-odds of diabetes. The default L2 regularization helps prevent overfitting on the relatively small dataset.

09 — Interpretation of Results

What do the metrics mean?

The model achieves an accuracy of 75.32% and an AUC of 0.8147 on the test set. The AUC is the more meaningful metric here — it measures how well the model ranks diabetic patients above non-diabetic ones, regardless of the classification threshold. A value of 0.81 indicates strong discriminative ability.

The precision for diabetic patients (0.649) means that when the model predicts diabetes, it is correct about 65% of the time. The recall of 0.673 means the model identifies 67.3% of all actual diabetic patients — missing about 1 in 3, which is the primary concern in a clinical setting.

Key observations

  • The model performs better at identifying non-diabetic patients (F1 = 0.806) than diabetic ones (F1 = 0.661), reflecting the class imbalance in the dataset (65% vs 35%).
  • Glucose and BMI are the strongest predictors of diabetes in this dataset.
  • The 18 false negatives are clinically significant — these are patients who have diabetes but were classified as healthy, delaying potential treatment.
  • Lowering the classification threshold (e.g., to 0.35) would increase recall at the cost of more false positives — a reasonable trade-off in medical screening.

Limitations

  • The dataset contains zeroes in fields like Glucose, BMI, and BloodPressure that likely represent missing values — these were not imputed in this implementation.
  • The dataset is limited to female patients of Pima Indian heritage, so the model may not generalize well to other populations.
  • A more advanced model (e.g. Random Forest, XGBoost) would likely improve recall on the diabetic class significantly.
