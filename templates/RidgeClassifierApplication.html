Student Dropout Prediction

Ridge Classifier applied to predict academic outcomes in higher education

01 — Problem Description

Student dropout is one of the most significant challenges in higher education institutions. Early identification of students at risk of dropping out allows universities to intervene proactively — offering academic support, counseling, or financial aid before the student abandons their studies.

This model uses a Ridge Classifier trained on academic performance and enrollment data to predict whether a student will Graduate / Stay Enrolled or Drop Out, based on their first and second semester results.

02 — Dataset

Predict Students' Dropout and Academic Success

The dataset contains records from students enrolled in undergraduate programs at a Portuguese higher education institution. It includes demographic, socioeconomic, and academic performance data collected at enrollment and at the end of the first two semesters.

04 — Data Preparation

01
Target encoding The original three-class target (Dropout, Enrolled, Graduate) was converted to binary: Dropout → 0, Graduate and Enrolled → 1. This focuses the model on the most critical distinction.
02
Feature selection 5 variables were selected from the original 34 features — those most directly related to academic performance and at-risk detection during the first year of study.
03
StandardScaler normalization All input features were standardized to zero mean and unit variance using StandardScaler. This is required for Ridge Classifier since it is sensitive to feature scale.
04
Train / Test split Data was split into 80% training (3,539 records) and 20% test (885 records) with random_state=42 for reproducibility.
05
Model training RidgeClassifier(alpha=1.0) was fitted on the scaled training set. Alpha=1.0 provides balanced regularization, preventing overfitting without over-constraining the model.

08 — Interpretation of Results

What the metrics tell us

The model achieves an accuracy of 79.5% — correctly classifying about 4 out of 5 students. The recall of 93.7% is particularly important in this context: it means the model correctly identifies the vast majority of students who will graduate or stay enrolled, minimizing the risk of failing to support them.

The AUC of {{ auc }} indicates strong discriminative ability — the model is significantly better than random at separating dropouts from graduates across all classification thresholds.

Reading the confusion matrix

  • 533 True Positives: students correctly predicted to graduate — the model's strongest area.
  • 171 True Negatives: dropouts correctly identified — useful for early intervention.
  • 145 False Positives: dropouts incorrectly predicted as graduates — these students may be missed by support programs.
  • 36 False Negatives: graduates incorrectly flagged as at-risk — low impact, leads to unnecessary but harmless interventions.

Limitations

  • Only 5 variables are used — adding socioeconomic and demographic features could improve accuracy.
  • The model is linear and may miss non-linear patterns in student behavior.
  • The class imbalance (68% graduate vs 32% dropout) may bias predictions toward the majority class.
